I have started newsletters before. I have started them on Substack, on Mailchimp, in a notes app I never opened again. Each time the problem was not that I ran out of things to say — it was that the platform felt like a performance. There was always a button shaped like a megaphone. There was always a view count. There was always a faint suggestion that the thing I was writing should be more legible, more consistent, more findable by the algorithm.

I don't want to write for the algorithm.

What I want is something closer to a long letter — the kind people used to send to a small number of people who had asked to receive them. Nothing optimised. Nothing promoted. Just writing, and photography, and the occasional thing that doesn't fit anywhere else.

## Why build it yourself

The short answer is that I wanted to own the thing completely. No platform deciding what the design looks like. No third party between me and the people who subscribe. If this site disappears, it will be because I turned it off, not because a company pivoted.

The slightly longer answer is that building it was interesting. It took an afternoon. It's a static site — just files — which means it is fast, cheap to host, and will probably still render correctly in twenty years. That appeals to me.

## What to expect

Irregular posts. Photography. Essays about small things I've been paying attention to. Possibly some notes on making things — code, food, images. Nothing topical. Nothing that requires you to already be paying attention to the news.

If that sounds useful to you, there's a subscribe form below. If you later decide it doesn't, unsubscribing is one click.

That's about it.
